## Proven Frameworks (Performance-Backed for This Account)

These frameworks have live performance data on Insight Links campaigns. They are the default starting point for any new concept batch.

### 1. Confession / Objection-First (Framework #4)

**Formula:** Proactively name the objection, then resolve it.

**Why it works for clinicians:** Medical professionals are trained skeptics. Leading with a vulnerability ("Confession:") bypasses their ad filter because it reads as peer-to-peer honesty, not a sales pitch. The format signals "I'm one of you" before it sells anything.

**Account evidence:** DCT_103_Luiz_Confession was the dominant performer across all three newsletters from January through mid-March 2026. Cardiac Wire: 691 leads L30D at $1.90 raw CPL. Imaging Wire: 503 leads L30D at $3.03 raw CPL.

**April usage notes:**
- DCT_103 is showing quality fatigue (Jake flagged non-target readers March 19). New Confession concepts must use fresh hooks, not reuse the original.
- Confession works best when the "confession" is a specific professional pain point, not a generic one. "Confession: I used to start every shift worried I'd missed something critical" > "Confession: I have too many emails."
- Pair with explicit role-qualifying language to protect quality ("If you're a cardiologist who..." / "If you work in radiology...").

**Adapted examples for April:**
- IW: "Confession: I'm a radiologist who used to start rounds unsure if I'd missed a study that changed the standard of care. Now I check one email."
- CW: "Confession: I'm a cardiologist and I don't have time to read journals anymore. This is what I do instead."
- DHW: "Confession: I run a health IT department and I was always the last person in the room to hear about a new regulation. Not anymore."

### 2. Text Callout Static (Pain-Point Amplification)

**Formula:** Isolate a single, specific pain point in plain text. No design — just the words.

**Why it works for clinicians:** This audience processes dense text professionally. A text-heavy static that names their exact frustration — with no visual clutter — reads like a colleague's note, not an ad. It survives the "is this spam?" filter that clinicians apply to everything in their inbox.

**Account evidence:** DCT_118_Text Call out broke out on Cardiac Wire: 318 leads at $1.29 raw CPL, 4.09% CTR, and 46 qualified leads in one week. It is both a volume AND quality driver — CW qualified leads jumped +53% WoW when DCT_118 dominated spend.

**April usage notes:**
- This is Insight Links' most important format right now. Every April batch should include at least 1-2 text callout statics.
- The winning pain point pattern: "feeling you need to read/be on top of everything all the time." April concepts should find new articulations of this same pressure.
- Keep it visually plain. No gradients, no stock photos, no logos. Black text on white, or white text on dark background. The uglier it looks, the more it bypasses ad filtering.

**Adapted examples for April:**
- IW: "You have 47 unread radiology journals. You will never read them. There is a faster way to know what matters."
- CW: "Every cardiologist I know has the same problem: too many studies, not enough time, and a constant feeling you're behind. One email fixes that."
- DHW: "You're responsible for buying healthcare software. The market moves weekly. You don't have time to track it. But you have to."

### 3. "If You, BUT" Conditional Hook

**Formula:** "If you're doing X, BUT [qualifying condition]..." — conditional framing that pre-qualifies the reader.

**Why it works for clinicians/executives:** The conditional structure self-selects the right audience before they even click. It says "this is for a specific person" — which appeals to professionals who are used to being targeted with irrelevant mass messaging. The "BUT" creates a micro-curiosity gap.

**Account evidence:** DCT_123_If you BUT delivered the best DHW CPL ever recorded: 143 leads at $1.51 raw CPL, 3.26% CTR. Jake approved expanding this to non-clinician DHW targeting for healthcare admin/IT buyers.

**April usage notes:**
- The highest-priority expansion area for April. Jake explicitly approved the "If you work in healthcare BUT you're not a clinician" frame for DHW.
- Each newsletter should get its own conditional variation targeting the specific ICP.
- The "BUT" clause is the qualifying mechanism. Make it specific enough to filter out non-target audiences (nurses, retired, patients, students).

**Adapted examples for April:**
- DHW (non-clinician): "If you work in healthcare BUT you're not a clinician and instead your job involves EHR systems, clinical software, or health IT, this newsletter is for you."
- IW: "If you're a radiologist BUT you've stopped reading journals because there's too much noise, this 5-minute email is what you've been looking for."
- CW: "If you're a cardiologist BUT you feel like you're always one study behind, you need to know about this."

### 4. Transformation / Before-After / Apple Notes (Framework #2 + Editorial Native)

**Formula:** Show the before state (information chaos) and after state (clarity via newsletter) using a raw, lo-fi format like Apple Notes screenshots.

**Why it works for clinicians:** The visual proof of transformation — from "drowning in journals" to "caught up in 5 minutes" — is concrete and believable. The Apple Notes format signals authenticity (this looks like something a colleague wrote, not a marketing team). This framework has the highest qualification rate in the account.

**Account evidence:** DCT_115_AppleNotesTransformation on DHW: 196 conversions at $3.22 raw CPL with an 82% qualification rate (L14D, among assessed leads). DCT_114_ReplaceJournals on IW: 194 leads at $2.39 raw CPL with a 75% qualification rate (L14D, among assessed leads).

**April usage notes:**
- This is the quality benchmark. When you need qualified leads over volume, use this framework.
- The CPL will be higher than Confession or Life Hack — that is acceptable. Jake explicitly values qualification rate over raw CPL.
- "Replace journals" is a validated angle for IW. April concepts should explore what else these newsletters replace (conference recaps, peer conversations, Twitter scrolling, specialty listservs).
- Use the Apple Notes visual format. It works because it looks undesigned.

**Adapted examples for April:**
- IW: Before: Screenshot of 20+ open browser tabs with radiology journal titles. After: One clean email with "The Imaging Wire" header. Apple Notes caption: "I replaced all of these with one email."
- CW: Apple Notes screenshot: "Things I stopped doing after subscribing to Cardiac Wire: 1. Checking 4 cardiology sites every morning 2. Feeling behind before rounds 3. Missing the study everyone else read"
- DHW: Before/After split: "Before: 45 minutes reading 6 health tech newsletters. After: 5 minutes reading Digital Health Wire."

### 5. Life Hack TOV (Framework #5 Listicle variant)

**Formula:** "Life hack for [specific role] trying to [specific professional goal]" — positions the newsletter as an efficiency tool.

**Why it works for clinicians:** Time is the scarcest resource for medical professionals. Framing the newsletter as a "life hack" (something that saves time with no trade-off) lowers the commitment barrier. It works because these professionals genuinely need to stay current but genuinely lack time.

**Account evidence:** DCT_104_Luiz_Life hack on CW: 513 leads L30D at $1.76 raw CPL, 3.75% CTR. On DHW: 426 leads L30D at $2.80 raw CPL.

**April usage notes:**
- Use as a volume anchor, not a quality driver. Qualification rates are historically lower with this format.
- Pair Life Hack concepts with Text Callout or "If you, BUT" concepts in the same ad set to balance volume and quality.
- Keep the "life hack" framing specific to role: "Life hack for radiologists" performs better than "Life hack for healthcare professionals" because specificity qualifies the audience.

**Adapted examples for April:**
- IW: "Life hack for radiologists trying to stay current without spending your entire lunch break reading journals."
- CW: "Life hack for cardiologists who need to know what happened in cardiology this week but have 6 minutes, not 60."
- DHW: "Life hack for health IT leaders who need to track digital health trends without adding another meeting to your calendar."

## Experimental Frameworks for April (Untested but High-Potential for B2B Healthcare)

These frameworks from the master playbook have not been tested on Insight Links but align well with the audience profile. Each one maps to a specific gap in the current creative mix.

### 6. Us vs Them / Comparison (Framework #3)

**Why to try it:** Clinicians are trained to evaluate evidence comparatively. A direct comparison between "the old way" (journals, Twitter, conference recaps) and "the new way" (one curated email) maps to how they already think.

**Fills this gap:** No current Insight Links creative uses explicit comparison framing. The closest is DCT_114_ReplaceJournals, but that is Before/After, not side-by-side.

**Example concepts:**
- "JAMA: 45 minutes, one specialty. The Imaging Wire: 5 minutes, everything that matters in radiology."
- "Other newsletters: general healthcare noise. Cardiac Wire: only cardiology. Only what moved this week."
- "Twitter: rumors, takes, noise. Digital Health Wire: verified, curated, 5 minutes."

**Risk level:** Low. The comparison must be factual (do not name competitor newsletters by name — compare against categories like "journals" or "Twitter"). Clinicians respect precision; vague comparisons will backfire.

### 7. Curiosity Gap (Framework #12)

**Why to try it:** Information gap hooks work on intelligent audiences when the gap is specific and professional. "There's one study everyone missed this week" is more compelling to a cardiologist than "You won't believe what happened."

**Fills this gap:** Current top performers lead with pain or confession. Curiosity Gap is a distinct emotional register — anticipation rather than relief.

**Example concepts:**
- IW: "There's one imaging study published this month that changes the standard of care. 80% of radiologists haven't read it yet. (The Imaging Wire covered it on Tuesday.)"
- CW: "The cardiology story everyone's talking about at conferences right now — and the 3 implications nobody's connecting."
- DHW: "One health IT regulation is about to change how your organization buys clinical software. Digital Health Wire readers already know about it."

**Risk level:** Medium. The curiosity gap MUST be resolved with real newsletter content — do not bait. Clinicians will disengage if the ad promises specificity and the newsletter delivers generalities. Only use this if the team can reference actual recent stories.

**Why to try it:** Insight Links has real institutional credibility — radiologists at named practices, cardiologists at hospital systems, health system CTOs. The bake-off data proves TFM delivers actual ICP professionals. This proof has not been weaponized in creative yet.

**Fills this gap:** No current Insight Links creative leads with social proof. All top performers lead with pain or confession. Social proof is an untested angle that could unlock a new audience segment (professionals who respond to peer validation rather than pain).

**Example concepts:**
- IW: "Read by radiologists at Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, and Johns Hopkins. There's a reason. (Free to join.)"
- CW: "12,000 cardiologists start their morning with Cardiac Wire. What do they know that you don't?"
- DHW: "The newsletter health IT leaders at [X] hospital systems read before their Monday standup."

**Risk level:** Medium. Must use real, verifiable proof points. Jake would need to approve any institutional name drops. Subscriber counts alone are weaker for this audience — role-specific proof (e.g., "read by 200 radiologists") is stronger than volume proof ("read by 50,000 people").

## Anti-Patterns: Frameworks to Avoid for Insight Links

### DO NOT USE: Meme/Culture Format (Framework #11)

**Why:** Medical professionals processing clinical information in a professional context will not engage with meme formats. It violates the brand voice rules (no "fluffy" or oversimplified content) and risks attracting non-qualified audiences (patients, students, general public) who share memes. The Insight Links audience needs to feel that the newsletter is curated by domain experts — memes undermine that credibility signal.

### DO NOT USE: UGC Testimonial with AI Avatars (Framework #9 variant)

**Why:** Healthcare is a high-trust category. The master frameworks file explicitly flags AI UGC as "Risky" for health and finance. Fake testimonials from AI-generated "doctors" would be both an FTC risk and a credibility disaster for a newsletter that serves actual clinicians. If UGC testimonial is used, it must be a real healthcare professional (or real subscriber), not an AI avatar.

### DO NOT USE: Reverse Psychology / "Don't Subscribe" Hooks

**Why:** Ironic framing reads as gimmicky to this audience. Clinicians' ad filter is calibrated to reject anything that feels like a marketing trick. "Don't subscribe to this newsletter" will be taken at face value and scrolled past. The brand voice explicitly requires straightforward, professional-but-accessible language.

### DO NOT USE: Generic Bold Promise Hooks ("Be the smartest person in the room")

**Why:** Clinicians already consider themselves experts. Promising to make them "smarter" or "the most informed" can feel patronizing. The validated approach is to frame the newsletter as a time-saver and noise-filter, not an intelligence upgrade. "Stay current without the burden" works. "Become the smartest cardiologist" does not.

### DO NOT USE: Consumer-Grade Urgency ("Limited time!" / "Sign up before midnight!")

**Why:** Jake's NEVER rules explicitly prohibit consumer-grade language. Urgency-based CTAs that work for B2C newsletters (countdown timers, scarcity framing) will make this audience disengage immediately. The CTA should be soft or curiosity-driven: "See what you're missing" or "Read this week's issue" — not "Join now before it's too late."

### DO NOT USE: Broad/Vague Hooks Without Role Specificity

**Why:** DCT_120_Information Overload confirmed this anti-pattern: $5.52 raw CPL, 0.84% CTR, paused after 3 weeks. "Information overload" is a universal pain point that attracts everyone — including non-qualified audiences. Every hook must name the role (radiologist, cardiologist, health IT leader) or the specialty (radiology, cardiology, digital health). Specificity is the qualifying mechanism.

## Quick Reference: April Concept Formula

For each new DCT concept, construct from these validated building blocks:

```
[HOOK: Confession / Text Callout / "If you, BUT" / Curiosity Gap / Subtraction]
+
[BODY: Role-specific pain > newsletter as filter/solution]
+
[PROOF: Qualification rate data, real subscriber roles, or content preview]
+
[CTA: Soft — "See what you're missing" / "Read this week's issue" / "Subscribe free"]
```

**Format priority for April:**
1. Text Callout Static (highest quality + volume balance)
2. Apple Notes / Transformation Static (highest qualification rate)
3. TOV — Text Over Video with Confession or Life Hack script (volume driver)
4. "If you, BUT" Static or TOV (highest pre-qualification)
5. Carousel of actual newsletter headlines (untested — low-risk experiment)

**Per-newsletter targeting language:**
- **IW:** "radiologist," "imaging," "radiology" — never generic "healthcare professional"
- **CW:** "cardiologist," "cardiology" — add age qualifier (25-59) to exclude retired
- **DHW (clinician):** "digital health," "health tech" — acceptable for clinical audience
- **DHW (non-clinician):** "EHR systems," "clinical software," "health IT," "healthcare admin" — per Jake's March 17 directive
